Introduction to Electric Vehicles

Electric Vehicles (EVs) aren't a new thing. Believe it or not, EVs have been around since the early 1800s. Around the year 1832, a gentleman by the name of Robert Anderson developed the first crude electric vehicle. It wasn't until the 1870s until battery technology advanced enough to make them practical. EVs were fighting a loosing battle however. They were dominated by their early Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) rivals due to current battery technology and length of time it took to charge them.

Fast forward to today, with modern battery technology, and the gap to charge an EV vs. fill up an ICE vehicle is drawing closer. My goal is to educate people about current EV technology, dispel myths about EVs, list the pros and cons of owning an EV, introduce you to the different types of EVs and help you choose a model that works with your driving needs and habits.

The most common myth is EVs have been labeled as dangerous because they can catch fire. While this is true with any type of vehicle, many safeguards have been put in place to make the possibility of this happening less likely. In a recent article published by CNN Business, the EV manufacture Tesla, claims that EVs are safer than their gasoline counterparts. According to Tesla's data of their own EVs, for every 1 billion miles traveled by a Tesla brand car, there were five EV fires. For gasoline cars, that rate worked out to 55 fires for every billion miles traveled. Operation costs are typically cheaper as well, depending on where in the country you live and the current electricity rate per kWh. Factor this in with no maintenance items like spark plugs, air filters, fuel filters or oil changes, and your cost to operate gets even cheaper. To determine which type of EV is right for you lets take a look at the types and list some basic differences.

Hybrid Electric Vehicle(HEV): A HEV uses and Internal Combustion Engine coupled with a battery. These vehicles don't plug in for an external charge. Instead they use an ICE to produce electricity with a generator which provides power to a battery, which is in turn connected to an electric motor to propel the vehicle. The battery can also capture energy when the vehicle is coasting down hill or stopping in a process call regenerative breaking. The most popular model that uses this technology is the Toyota Prius.

Plug-In Hybrid Electric Vehicle(PHEV): A PHEV uses the same technology as a HEV with one unique difference. PHEVs have larger capacity batteries and can be plugged in to an external voltage source to be charged. This allows the the vehicle to operate in a completely emissions free battery only mode. Once the battery charge is depleted the gasoline ICE will operate and the vehicle then functions like an HEV until recharged. Popular models that use this technology is the Chevrolet Volt and the Chrysler Pacifica minivan.

Battery Electric Vehicles(BEV): A BEV relies on a battery only. These types of vehicles usually have the largest batteries since they have no gasoline engine. These are truly emissions free vehicles producing zero emissions. The limiting factor of a BEV is the amount of time it takes to charge the larger size battery packs. There are different levels and methods of charging EVs. Depending on how much a person drives and the amount of time they have to recharge is the limiting factor for most people with this type of EV. We will learn more about the different charging methods and times as we progress. The popular models that are BEV only are the Tesla Model S, Model X and Model 3, the Nissan Leaf, the Fiat 500e, and the Chevrolet Bolt.
